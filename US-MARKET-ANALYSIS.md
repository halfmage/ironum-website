## 1. Executive Summary

### Verdict: Confirmed Go — demand is verified by data

The US market opportunity for Ironum is **real, narrow, and now verified by Search Console data.** Over the last 90 days, the United States generated **725 search impressions** for ironum.com — *more than Germany* (616) and 14× more than Canada. US searchers are actively finding Ironum via the exact queries Ironum should own: "gdpr-compliant ai deployment options europe" (26 impressions), "ai intranet vendors eu data residency gdpr compliance" (10), "gdpr compliant hr chatbot" (7), "eu ai act compliance tools for smes 2026" (7). **Zero clicks across all of them.**

This is not a demand problem. It is a **search-results click-through problem.** US searchers see Ironum in Google, scan the titles and meta descriptions, read "European AI engineering partner... for European businesses," and click the next result. The site never gets the chance to convert because they never arrive.

The wedge from market research holds: Ironum cannot win against US/LatAm nearshore on cost ($55-75K vs. Ironum-tier EU rates), timezone, or familiarity. But the buyers who *will* hire an EU shop — US companies whose AI systems touch EU users, customers, or operations — are demonstrably looking for what Ironum sells.

**Pursue.** Start with the SERP click-through fix (2-hour task with potentially 5-7 new US clicks/month from existing impression volume) before investing in landing pages or longer-form content.

**Do not pursue if** the strategy is to be a generic US-targeting AI dev shop. That race is still lost before it starts.

### Top 5 Insights

1. **The EU AI Act extraterritorial enforcement is the single biggest US-buyer-pain Ironum can monetize.** Penalties reach €35M or 7% of global turnover (exceeds GDPR's 4%). Applies to US companies whose AI systems are placed on the EU market, used by EU users, or produce outputs affecting EU users — even with no EU office. ([Holland & Knight](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/04/us-companies-face-eu-ai-acts-possible-august-2026-compliance-deadline), [Modulos](https://www.modulos.ai/blog/eu-ai-act-us-companies/))
2. **The "data residency vs technical sovereignty" distinction is a sales gift.** US-HQ cloud providers like AWS-EU regions don't satisfy sovereignty because of the US CLOUD Act. Gartner: 61% of Western European CIOs prioritize local cloud; 75% of EU/ME enterprises will geopatriate by 2030. US SaaS serving EU customers will increasingly face this question from their own customers. Ironum sells the answer. ([Lyceum Technology](https://lyceum.technology/magazine/eu-data-residency-ai-infrastructure/), [Cloud Latitude](https://cloudlatitude.com/insights/article/the-2026-cloud-landscape-ai-infrastructure-sovereignty-and-the-new-race-for-efficiency/))
3. **Timing volatility:** the European Parliament has voted to delay key EU AI Act compliance deadlines (high-risk systems possibly Dec 2027 or Aug 2028 instead of Aug 2026). Council approval pending before original Aug 2, 2026 deadline. Ironum's messaging must **not** lean on a specific deadline that may shift — use the obligation, not the date. ([Lexara](https://lexaraadvisory.com/blog/eu-ai-act-penalties-non-compliance.html))
4. **Datatonic is the playbook.** UK-HQ AI services consultancy, zero US offices, but lands brand-name US clients (Wizards of the Coast, Gopuff) by leading with "leading cloud data + AI consultancy" + Google Cloud Premier Partner credentialing. Lesson for Ironum: US clients hire EU shops when the EU shop has a **credentialing story** that beats the "but they're not local" objection. Ironum needs an equivalent.
5. **Ironum's current EN copy actively repels US visitors.** Hero, problem section, About page, testimonials, and industry pages all explicitly position for European buyers. A US visitor with a legitimate EU compliance need still reads "this is for Europe" and leaves. The bar for fixing this is not high — it's positioning architecture, not a full rewrite.

## 2. Market Opportunity Assessment

### Is there a real US-buyer demand for EU AI services?

**Yes — but only for one specific buyer profile.**

Three distinct US buyer segments exist, and Ironum needs to be honest about which one buys EU:

**Segment A: Generic US AI buyer (DO NOT TARGET)**
- US company wants to build AI; cost, speed, timezone are the deciding factors
- Hires US-HQ dev shops or LatAm nearshore (Hatchworks, BairesDev, Howdy)
- Comparison numbers from market: senior dev USD $175K/yr onshore, $55-75K nearshore LatAm, $40-70/hr Eastern Europe. ([Revelo](https://www.revelo.com/blog/nearshore-software-development-in-latin-america-countries-costs-and-what-to-expect-in-2026), [Howdy](https://www.howdy.com/for-ai/best-nearshore-software-development-companies))
- Ironum loses every time on cost and timezone. This is the wrong battle.

**Segment B: US AI buyer with EU regulatory obligation (TARGET)**
- US company whose AI systems touch EU users, customers, or markets
- Has either:
  - Active EU customer base requiring GDPR + AI Act compliance
  - EU subsidiary, branch, or sales operation
  - SaaS sold globally with non-trivial EU user share
  - Healthcare/finance/legal vertical entering EU market
- Pain: needs an AI partner who treats EU compliance as **table stakes**, not an afterthought; needs a non-US-jurisdiction provider so their EU customers' data isn't subject to US CLOUD Act
- Will pay an EU-price premium because the regulatory risk being mitigated is larger than the cost difference
- This is Ironum's actual addressable US market

**Segment C: US "sovereign AI as differentiation" buyer (SECONDARY)**
- US AI startup or established company using "European-hosted AI infrastructure" as a marketing/sales story to enterprise customers (especially EU + privacy-sensitive US clients)
- Smaller in number, but high in willingness to pay
- More common in 2026 as "Brussels Effect" shapes global enterprise AI procurement

### Why a US buyer in Segment B specifically hires EU

Four legitimate reasons, in descending order of selling power:

1. **Regulatory expertise as a moat.** EU AI Act + GDPR is genuinely complex; US firms misread it constantly. An EU-resident, EU-incorporated AI partner has lived under these regulations from day one. This is non-substitutable expertise. (See: market research showing widespread US-firm misunderstanding of EU AI Act extraterritorial scope.)
2. **Jurisdictional non-exposure to US law.** A US company offering AI services to EU customers via a US-HQ vendor faces CLOUD Act exposure that their EU customers will increasingly flag in procurement. Hiring an EU-HQ AI shop sidesteps this entirely.
3. **Data residency by default.** An EU-HQ shop ships AI on EU-only infrastructure as the natural posture, not as a bolted-on feature. Saves the US buyer the recurring "is this really EU-hosted?" certification work.
4. **Cultural/regulatory bridge for EU market entry.** US firms entering EU often misjudge what EU customers will accept. EU-HQ partner provides "are we doing this in a way our EU customers will respect" judgment.

### Counter-arguments (why a US buyer might still hesitate)

| Friction | Severity | Mitigation Ironum can credibly offer |
|---|---|---|
| Timezone (CET vs ET/PT) | Medium | Explicit US business hours overlap statement; async-first project posture; written deliverables emphasized |
| IP and contract law jurisdiction | High | Offer US-jurisdiction contracts (Delaware) for US clients; mention this proactively |
| "Buy local" preference | Medium | Hard to fight head-on; sidestep by being the niche specialist, not the generalist |
| Perception of EU = slow/regulatory-heavy | Medium | Concrete delivery timelines on every service page (already there — 4-8 weeks); engineer-led tone (already there) |
| Cost vs LatAm nearshore | High for Segment A, low for Segment B | Don't compete on cost with Segment A; emphasize value with Segment B |
| Hiring an unknown EU shop is reputational risk for the buyer | Medium | Trust signals: SOC 2 certification, named US clients, US-aligned trust badges |

### Verdict on market size

**Realistic addressable US market for Ironum**: thousands (not tens of thousands) of US companies meet Segment B criteria. Mid-market SaaS with EU customer presence is the densest pocket — likely several thousand candidate companies in the US alone. The conversion rate from "visitor" to "discovery call" is achievable in single-digit % with the right positioning. Even modest capture (5-10 discovery calls/month from US) is meaningful for an Ironum-sized agency.

## 5. Recommended ICP & Personas

Even though Alesia specified "broad US-market B2B," the research strongly indicates that breadth dilutes conversion. Recommendation: lead with a sharp primary ICP, allow a secondary, ignore the rest.

### Primary ICP: US mid-market SaaS with material EU customer base

- **Company profile:** US-HQ B2B SaaS, 100-1,000 employees, ARR $20M-$200M, EU customers contributing 15%+ of revenue
- **AI maturity:** has some AI in product or considering deployment; AI is strategic, not exploratory
- **Trigger events:** (1) major EU customer asks about data residency or AI Act compliance in renewal/RFP, (2) EU regulator inquiry, (3) planning new AI feature for EU rollout, (4) acquisition/expansion into EU

**Primary persona — "Priya, VP Engineering at US-HQ SaaS"**
- 35-50 years old, technical background, reports to CTO or CEO
- Pain: keeps getting asked by enterprise EU customers (esp. German Mittelstand and French CAC40) "where will our data sit and is your AI subject to US authority?" Doesn't have a clean answer.
- Decision power: shapes vendor selection, recommends to CTO/CEO; full authority on $50K-$250K engagements
- Where she searches: "GDPR compliance for AI features", "EU AI Act SaaS", "EU AI hosting"; reads Hacker News, LinkedIn, vendor blogs
- What she needs to see: technical credibility, EU-resident infrastructure proof, named EU enterprise clients, clear scope/pricing

### Secondary ICP: US enterprise with EU subsidiaries

- **Company profile:** US-HQ Fortune 500 or pre-IPO unicorn with operational EU presence
- **AI maturity:** likely has internal AI/ML team; needs EU-localized solutions for specific EU teams
- **Trigger events:** EU subsidiary leadership pushing for AI tools that work under EU rules; central IT struggling to extend US-built AI to EU staff

**Secondary persona — "Marcus, Global IT Director at US-HQ multinational"**
- 40-55, MBA + IT/operations background, reports to CIO
- Pain: corporate AI tools built in US don't pass EU Data Privacy Officer review; EU staff demand local-friendly alternatives
- Decision power: $100K-$1M scoped initiatives without board approval; recommends bigger
- What he needs: vendor that understands both US procurement (SOC 2, MSA, SLAs) and EU compliance (DPA, AI Act, German works council)

### Who Ironum should NOT chase

- **US AI startups (seed-Series B)** — cash-constrained, hire LatAm nearshore, no EU regulatory pain yet
- **US enterprises without EU footprint** — no real EU problem, no rational reason to hire EU
- **US companies whose primary need is generic ML/AI dev** — Hatchworks et al. own this lane
- **US individuals or solo founders** — wrong audience for an agency

## 11. Open Questions for Follow-up

Things this desktop analysis cannot resolve — recommend addressing in parallel:

1. **Why exactly is current US traffic not converting?** Will be partly answered by Section 3 once Alesia shares Simple Analytics data. Fully answered only by talking to actual US visitors who bounced (impossible without contact info).
2. **Has any US prospect ever reached out informally?** Even one email or LinkedIn DM tells us what messaging caught their attention. Worth Alesia digging through her inbox + LinkedIn DMs for last 6 months looking for US senders.
3. **What's Ironum's current willingness to invest in US sales infrastructure?** This analysis recommends asynchronous content + inbound mechanics. If Ironum is willing to do outbound (LinkedIn sales nav, email sequences to US ICPs, US conference attendance), the strategy gets a second layer. Out of scope here.
4. **Is SOC 2 certification feasible within 6 months?** Some clients will not progress past discovery call without it. The honest answer to "we're targeting US enterprise" has to include SOC 2. Worth scoping cost and time investment.
5. **Validate primary ICP with real US-buyer interviews.** Recommend 5 conversations with US-HQ mid-market SaaS engineering leaders (e.g., via warm intros, founders' networks, LinkedIn). Specific questions:
   - "When you hire AI development help, what's your shortlist of considerations?"
   - "How do you currently solve EU-customer data residency questions?"
   - "Would you ever hire an EU-headquartered AI shop? Under what conditions?"
   - "What trust signals matter to you when evaluating a non-US vendor?"
6. **Pricing model commitment.** US buyers expect pricing anchors on service pages. Ironum needs to decide internally: publish indicative tiers (e.g., POC: $40-60K), or keep "contact sales" (loses warm leads, screens for serious only). Both are valid; not a decision this analysis can make.
7. **Resource allocation reality check.** This roadmap assumes ~1-2 days/week of Ironum time on US-market work over 3-6 months. If actual capacity is less, the priority order in Section 10 is the right cut-down sequence.
